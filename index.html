Urban Transformation is a large scale project that intentionally transforms a location giving it the ability to add value to an area. Urban transformation takes into consideration climate change, demographics, sustainability, how the space will be used and potentially other factors.

The Development of Barangaroo

Barangaroo is defined as a “large-scale urban development”(http://www.barangaroo.com/home.aspx) Over half of the sector will be filled with public spaces, and a continuation of Sydney Harbour promenade, expansive parklands, plazas and coves. (https://www.barangaroo.com/the-project/progress/barangaroo-development/) Open Spaces for the public. A new water area will cover 50% of the 22 hectare site. (http://www.barangaroo.com/home.aspx)

Echo

A complete transformation of the location. It is divided into three precincts, Barangaroo South, Central and Reserve. The south is dedicated for the financial services and include apartments, a major hotel, shops, cafes, restaurants and cultural spaces.(http://www.barangaroo.com/home.aspx) A link dedicated to foot traffic will connect Barangaroo South with public transport at Wynyard and the city centre, thus making the area accessible. Barangaroo Central is endeavoured to create networks between commercial and community spaces and the Barangaroo Reserve is dedicated into becoming a “new destination” to enjoy the scenery of Sydney Harbour. (http://www.cityofsydney.nsw.gov.au/vision/changing-urban-precincts/barangaroo)

Integrated

It is projected to support 23,000 permanent jobs, home 3,500 residents and add $2 billion revenue to the New South Wales economy (https://www.barangaroo.com/the-project/progress/barangaroo-development/) Barangaroo is aimed to be Australia’s first large scale carbon neutral community, capable of being water positive and targeting zero net waste to landfill by 2020. Reducing the Co2 emission reduces the impact on the environment and climate change as a result. (https://www.barangaroosouth.com.au/about/~/media/Developments/AU/BR/Images/About/SustainabilityClimate%20Change%20Adaptation%20%20Community%20Resilience.pdf)

The species selection is meticulous and botanically expansive. There are over 70,000 plants in total, with some never before used in a horticultural setting. Stuart Pittendrigh, responsible for the planting, remarks that the “growth rates have been spectacular” and less than 1% of specimens have been lost during their establishment. These outstanding growth rates can largely be attributed to a rigorous technical approach to phosphorous, pH and permeability or drainage, established by soil specialist Simon Leake.
