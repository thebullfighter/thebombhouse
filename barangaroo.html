Barangaroo

Development of Barangaroo

Barangaroo is currently under development with a current goal to be fully completed and developed by 2024. It was formerly known as Darling Harbour east, located along the harbour between King Street Wharf and Walsh Bay and unlike what it looks like today, Barangaroo succumbed to the 1960s the shipping container revolution which created a vast, featureless concrete apron, obliterating the original topography of the landscape. (ref) As technology advanced into the 21st century, the area was unable to adapt thus unsustainable. The NSW Government decided to redeveloped the area in 2003, into a new urban precinct and is currently supervised by the New South Wales state government. (ref)

Defining Barangaroo

Barangaroo is defined as a “large-scale urban development” (ref) Over half of the sector will be filled with public spaces, and a continuation of Sydney Harbour promenade, expansive parklands, plazas and coves. (ref) Open Spaces for the public. A new water area will cover 50% of the 22 hectare site. (ref) A complete transformation of the location. It is divided into three precincts, Barangaroo South, Central and Reserve. The south is dedicated for the financial services and include apartments, a major hotel, shops, cafes, restaurants and cultural spaces. (ref) A link dedicated to foot traffic will connect Barangaroo South with public transport at Wynyard and the city centre, thus making the area accessible. Barangaroo Central is endeavoured to create networks between commercial and community spaces and the Barangaroo Reserve is dedicated into becoming a “new destination” to enjoy the scenery of Sydney Harbour. (ref) It is projected to support 23,000 permanent jobs, home 3,500 residents and add $2 billion revenue to the New South Wales economy (ref) Barangaroo is aimed to be Australia’s first large scale carbon neutral community, capable of being water positive and targeting zero net waste to landfill by 2020. Reducing the Co2 emission reduces the impact on the environment and climate change as a result. (ref)

The Arguments

There was no real argument against the Barangaroo redevelopment, as the space before was outdated, and not used effectively. The NSW Government decided to turn the featureless landscape into a new thriving area. The main controversy revolves around the still in development, Barangaroo South. The area is aimed to contain many new and innovative buildings, the most prominent being the Crown Casino. The structure is aimed to be one of sydney's highest buildings, and also the first 6 star rated hotel in sydney. In 2016, there was a legal challenge from a community group, as there was an objection to the construction of the casino, as the land previously reserved for parklands. (ref) However, recently Crown Resorts has filed a lawsuit to stop the NSW government from approving developments that obstruct views of the Sydney Opera House and Harbour Bridge from its new Barangaroo hotel and casino. Lendlease have also taken legal action, as it affects some of their apartments as well. However, the Barangaroo Delivery Authority have been acting within their contractual obligations, and have negotiated with the Crown and Lendlease for 28 months.
